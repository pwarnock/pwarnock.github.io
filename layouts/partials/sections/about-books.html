Books That Shaped Me

These books have fundamentally influenced how I think about engineering, leadership, and building organizations:

Thinking, Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman (2011)

Understanding cognitive biases and heuristics transformed how I approach system design and team decision-making. It explains why we make predictable mistakes and how to account for human fallibility in processes.

Psychology Decision-Making Systems Thinking

The Phoenix Project

Gene Kim, Kevin Behr, George Spafford (2013)

A narrative about DevOps principles and continuous improvement. It showed me how traditional IT operations mindset fails at scale and why treating software delivery as a system—not a project—changes everything.

DevOps Systems Leadership

Accelerate

Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble, Gene Kim (2018)

Data-driven insights into what actually makes high-performing engineering teams. This book replaced intuition with metrics, showing that culture, practices, and architecture are measurable—and that measurement drives improvement.

Metrics Culture Performance

Poke the Box

Seth Godin (2011)

A manifesto on initiative and shipping. "All it takes is initiative. Even just a bit." This book grounded my philosophy that action beats perfectionism, and that starting is the only way to learn.

Leadership Shipping Initiative

Team of Rivals

Doris Kearns Goodwin (2005)

Lincoln's cabinet of competing egos and perspectives taught me that diverse viewpoints—even contentious ones—make organizations stronger. It changed how I hire, build teams, and navigate organizational complexity.

Leadership Teams History

The Mythical Man-Month

Fred Brooks (1975)

Timeless wisdom about software projects, team dynamics, and why "adding manpower to a late software project makes it later." Still the most important book on software engineering.

Software Engineering Teams Estimation
